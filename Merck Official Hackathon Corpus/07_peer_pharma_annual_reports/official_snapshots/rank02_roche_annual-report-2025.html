Discover how Roche made an impact in 2025

We built momentum in 2025 with our combined strength in pharmaceuticals and diagnostics, delivering progress for patients and pioneering new treatment options.

Explore our journey in the Annual Report

What if we could help more people live longer, healthier lives? What if we could preserve fading memories? What if we could spend more time with the people we love?

At Roche, we aim to prevent, stop and cure diseases. We built momentum in 2025 with our combined strength and pharmaceuticals and diagnostics delivering progress for patients and pioneering new treatment options. We advanced ten potentially life-changing new medicines into final development and delivered three important clinical late-stage successes in a row for breast cancer, multiple sclerosis and lupus. Overall, we have achieved 12 positive late-stage readouts in 2025. Not just adding years, but filling those years with health and quality.

Our AI algorithms in Diagnostics sharpen disease detection so patients can be diagnosed earlier than ever before. Our next-generation sequencing technology decoded an entire human genome in less than four hours – a world record.

We are tackling healthcare challenges where our impact matters most in areas such as oncology, neurology, and cardiovascular-metabolic diseases. Conditions in areas like these place the greatest strain on patients, families and healthcare systems around the world. We're partnering with patients to amplify voices, and we are collaborating with the brightest minds in science and healthcare, And we further reduced emissions by achieving our 100% sustainable electricity goal across our sites.

We have a deep commitment to our people, culture and values. We're bringing hope, care and health to people around the world. More memories. More moments. We're writing the next chapter, together.

Inside Science: exploring Alzheimer’s breakthroughs together

Alzheimer’s is a leading cause of death worldwide, creating a significant burden on healthcare systems, families and aging individuals.

Read our strategy

Alzheimer's is the seventh leading cause of death in the world. It is a huge burden to health care systems, but also to families and to people who are aging. Alzheimer's really, robs people of their identity. It takes away their memories, their personality and how they think and even how they perceive the world. So you can imagine it's very challenging.

Over 75% of Alzheimer's cases today are not diagnosed. And a typical diagnosis takes close to three years. So these are big challenges that we have faced. If you treat people late you may prevent progression of the disease. But a lot of damages actually happen. I think it's important to move much earlier, to treat people in the stage when they're asymptomatic.

And so how do you test people when they're asymptomatic? You have to have a very simple, test to do that and to be able to rule out, Alzheimer's in the early stage.

When memory fades, science brings hope

The first signs were just whispers, like when my dad bought enough sugar to bake a thousand cookies. Then he forgot the bank password he’d used for decades. That’s when I knew. The Alzheimer’s diagnosis showed me how fragile life can be.

But it also gave us time to prepare, to make the most of every day. The hardest part is that it doesn’t just take memories. It can take the person you love. It’s the relationships and connections that make people who they are.

The work we do at Roche to help millions of families have more time together means so much to me. That is our ultimate hope.

When the medicine stops working

Antimicrobial resistance is a reality. It's already happening and is progressing.

Every surgery is done using antibiotics, and we can use antibiotics because they still work. But little by little, bacteria and other germs are learning to survive in this environment that we have created to protect us.

In order to find new antibiotics that can treat these infections. We need to come up with completely new mechanisms of action, completely new medicines.

That is why we need innovation. We need to find new mode of action, an antibiotic that is completely different from what was existing so far. The bacteria cannot develop resistance in a simple months or years.

The digital path: how digital technology and AI are redefining pathology and disease detection

Pathology is, I would say, the centre of the medicine. Most of the diagnosis, and even more important, most of the treatment plans for the patient go through pathology.

My work involves the diagnosis, the understanding of the diseases by studying the cells, only cells. I love pathology in all senses. I would say every day more. We are living an exciting moment in pathology.

I think Pamplona is a very nice city, a mix of old and modernity. When I have time, I like to walk to the centre of the city. and Plaza del Castillo is the real heart with plenty of people, but it's quiet, it's nice, it has history, and it's relaxing.

My name is Dr. Lozano, but everybody calls me Lola. I'm a pathologist, specialising in cytopathology and lung pathology. I'm the Director of Pathology here at the University of Navarra in Pamplona.

Everything that helps us to make better diagnosis will improve the healthcare system. With digital pathology, we can be more accurate, more precise, and can give some extra information to our clinician. and digital tools are very important for that.

Before having digital pathology, we have to send, physically send the samples for consultation. That means that some cases can even be lost or can be damaged.

When the sample comes to the department, the technician puts a sticker on it to identify the samples. Then the samples go to the grossing. The resident or the pathologist manipulates the samples in order to make a paraffin block. The samples get fixed in formalin. The next day, the technicians make this block and cut this block with the microtome. They make very thin sections. Then the sections go to the automatic stainer. And now with the digital from the stainer, they go directly to digitalisation. In our experience, digital pathology increases the pathology's productivity. We save time.

With the use of digital pathology, we can share cases with more expert pathologists to be more accurate and to ask, in some cases, especially in difficult cases, for help. One important thing also with digital pathology is the possibility to apply digital algorithms that helps us to give more specific information that doesn't rely anymore on the subjectivity of our eyes in the microscope. So, it relies on that well-established algorithms that help us to give the clinician a more exact information. It was impossible before.

My hope for digital pathology, probably in the future, it would help us to predict something that our eye is unable to detect.

Resilience and commitment: insights into the multiple sclerosis (MS) community

My name is Thomas Strube. I am in my mid-50s and in April 2018 I was diagnosed with MS. That was a severe blow of fate for me. From one hundred to zero within just a few months, which led to me ending up in a wheelchair. I could not accept that and then decided: You have to tackle this issue. I wanted to be able to walk again and together with my neurologist and my physiotherapist I then made the decision: You have to take a different path. You have to do sports, you need to get proper medication. You need something that gives you security.

Which led to me being able to walk again, that the relapses stopped, that I feel good. I feel healthy and I can walk. That is the most important thing for me and the second most important thing, that I was able to go back to work. This worked out in a way where, after six years, I was finally able to start working again. And it enabled me to live self-determined and to participate again in life and in social life and also in social interaction.

My experience has shown me that I can support others. That I must support them. That is why five years ago I founded a self-help group in Wolfenbüttel. All members of the group, now 27 in total, have decided to go through life together with others and also to make their story public. As a result, we have appropriate forums and can also be found in the newspapers. My role as group leader is to support the members of the group. All members can come to me at any time, receive support in all matters, whether these are health-related questions or whether it is simply a magazine they need to be able to look something up.

After my diagnosis, I searched the internet and immediately came across the website trotz MS under the DMSG (German Multiple Sclerosis Society). I still recommend these two websites today to people with MS, whether digital or in print. I believe everyone feels well supported there. I also think it is very strong that we now have digital applications, for example the BRISA app: to obtain information from the internet, to get it from the app, to be able to reflect your feelings there. To track how I felt a few weeks ago and to prepare myself for the next appointment with the neurologist. Today, as a patient and advocate, I make sure that other people with MS can follow, as far as possible, the same path as I have. That is what I wish for everyone.
